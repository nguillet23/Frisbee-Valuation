// The Data Behind the Disc

How We Built a
Crystal Ball

A deep dive into the methodology, findings, and implications of building a machine learning model that predicts offensive efficiency in professional Ultimate Frisbee.

The Problem

Offensive efficiency (OEFF) is one of the most important team-level metrics in competitive Ultimate Frisbee — but it's a lagging indicator. By the time a team knows their OEFF is suffering, the damage is done. The question we set out to answer: can we predict OEFF from individual player statistics before the season ends?

"If we can identify which player behaviors drive team offensive efficiency, coaches can make targeted adjustments mid-season rather than reacting after the fact."

The Data

We collected player statistics from 2021 through 2025, covering hundreds of player-seasons across the professional league. After applying a minimum games filter (G > 3) to remove noise from low-sample players, we had 312 clean records spanning 14 candidate features including completions, assists, turnovers, and plus/minus.

Null values were encoded as several placeholder strings ('--', 'N/A', 'none') in the source data, requiring careful preprocessing before any modeling could begin.

Feature Selection

Not all statistics are created equal. We first filtered by correlation with OEFF using a threshold of |r| ≥ 0.20, removing features with little predictive signal. We then used Random Forest feature importance to rank surviving features, selecting the top 8 for forward selection search.

The result was striking: plus/minus differential (+/- ▼) was far and away the most important single predictor (importance: 0.342), followed by opportunities (OPP) and scoring touches (SCR). This makes intuitive sense — players with high plus/minus are on the field during more successful offensive possessions.

The top three features — plus/minus, opportunities, and scoring touches — account for 79.3% of total model importance.

Model Selection

Five model types were evaluated: Linear Regression, Ridge, Lasso, Random Forest, and Gradient Boosting. Random Forest led on cross-validated R² (0.870) with Gradient Boosting close behind (0.824). Linear models underperformed, suggesting non-linear relationships between player stats and team OEFF.

Forward feature selection on the top 8 features across three model architectures produced the final winner: a Random Forest on 6 features, achieving a cross-validated R² of 0.870 and in-sample R² of 0.923.

What the Residuals Tell Us

The residual distribution is roughly centered at zero with a standard deviation of ~0.031, indicating no systematic bias. The top over-performers (actual OEFF exceeding predictions by 0.06+) trend toward handlers — suggesting that handler-specific intangibles may not be fully captured by the current feature set.

Conversely, top under-performers trend toward cutters, pointing to a clear next step: position-specific sub-models.

What's Next

The model is currently trained on aggregate season statistics. Future work should explore rolling-window mid-season predictions, position-specific models, opponent-adjusted statistics, and integration with real-time throw data if available.
